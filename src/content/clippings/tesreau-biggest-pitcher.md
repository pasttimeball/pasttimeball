With the 1913 World's Series days away, the El Paso Herald carried a syndicated series called Men Who Will Fight For the World's Championship, and this installment introduces Jeff Tesreau of the Giants under the finest job title the paper could bestow: "Charles M. Tesreau, the Biggest Pitcher In the World." He is drawn accordingly, a head the size of a watermelon on a windmilling body, flinging a baseball that leaves a trail of spray.

The spray is the point. "Charles M. Tesreau," the caption explains, "depends upon moisture for his pitching success." That is the polite way a family newspaper describes a spitball, and in 1913 it was an honest living. The biographical notes fill in the rest: "They call him the Ozark bear hunter because the only bear he ever saw was in a circus," illustrated with a boy gawking at a bear cage, and, in the corner, a personal item of real actuarial insight: "He is not yet married but he has an automobile so he should be soon."

The credit line under the panel reads "(By Sloane Gordon)," but the signature inked in the grass says Scar, the same hand that signed the How Great Men Play the Game series the Herald carried all that summer. Whichever name the syndicate put on the check, he had the essentials of Tesreau down: big man, wet ball, dry wit.
