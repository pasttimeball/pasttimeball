The Associated Press moved a small item out of Chicago on December 12, 1929: "School children may be admitted for 25 cents each to all major league base ball games in 1930 excepting those played on Saturdays, Sundays and holidays."

The plan had respectable sponsorship. "At the suggestion of Kenesaw M. Landis, commissioner of base ball, Alderman Thomas J. Bowler of Chicago was bound for the joint meeting of the majors at New York today with the plan of cut-rate tickets for youngsters." A city alderman carrying the commissioner's idea to the owners is a nice piece of political engineering, and Bowler came armed with the argument: "The youngsters are losing interest in base ball because they can't pay high prices for tickets," he said. "If the rate is cut to 25 cents for them, they will become interested in the game again."

Every generation of baseball men rediscovers this exact worry, that the game is losing the kids, and every generation's proposed cure is the same, get them through the gate cheap and let the game do the rest. Bowler's version has the virtue of a hard number. Two bits for a future fan, weekdays only, since Saturdays, Sundays and holidays drew fine without help.

The date on the page adds its own footnote. In December 1929 the country was six weeks past the stock market's great faceplant, and a quarter was about to mean a good deal more to a school child's family than it had meant in the spring. The owners were being asked to bet two bits on the long term. This site's readers may draw their own conclusions about how eagerly a room full of owners reaches for the long term. Elsewhere on the same sports page, the Star ran [a full cartoon on the White Sox first baseman then moonlighting as a prizefighter](/clippings/the-fighting-first-baseman/), so the kids were missing a colorful product.
