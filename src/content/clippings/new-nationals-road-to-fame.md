On St. Patrick's Day 1912 the Washington Times gave most of the top of its sports page to a cartoon signed Peene, headed "Retrieving Lost Laurels in Second Game, New Nationals Now Start on Road to Fame." The Nationals were in Charlottesville playing the University of Virginia into shape, they had evidently dropped the first meeting, and the second one went much better.

The scoring runs vignette by vignette. Virginia's whole offense gets one panel: "Finlay owns the town. He scored Virginia's only run," with the hero riding a wave of collegians. The rest of the college's afternoon is a professor in a V gown standing under a window while a grinning ballplayer in a W sweater empties a bucket over him, captioned "nearly whitewashed." A pitcher advises a batter, "gentle freshman, as we remark in Spain - strike two." Ainsmith's baserunning gets an asterisk drawn as a boot: "Ainsmith 'stole' third just like this (he's s'out)." And "Akers and Boehling make good," buried in thrown bouquets.

The best gag is dated exactly to the newspaper it ran in. Three Nationals march in a row wearing giant shamrocks lettered 1 BASE, 2 BASE and 3 BASE under a pennant reading BATTING HONORS: "Milan, McBride and Cunningham right there with the triple decoration." Three-baggers as St. Patrick's Day regalia. Some jokes only work one morning a year, and this cartoon made its deadline.
