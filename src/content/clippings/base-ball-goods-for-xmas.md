Nine days before Christmas 1908, Hood & Curlin of Waxahachie laid out the case with the calm of men stating a law of nature: "It's just a matter of a few weeks now when every healthy American boy will be playing Base Ball for dear life. He will need" and then the list, each line in bold, "A Good Base Ball. A Good Base Ball Bat. A Good Base Ball Glove. and probably A Good Catcher's Mit and Catcher's Mask."

That "and probably" is the honest heart of the ad. The ball, bat and glove are certainties. The catcher's gear is a probability, because somebody's boy is going to end up behind the plate whether he asked to or not, and a mask under the tree beats a Christmas visit to the doctor.

The stock had just landed: "We have received today our big shipment of 'Spalding' Base Ball goods and 'Spalding's' Goods are the acknowledged best." Hood & Curlin were prescription druggists by trade, "First in Everything" by motto, and they saw no contradiction in filling your prescription and outfitting your infield from the same counter. Ask them to show you the line. They asked first.
