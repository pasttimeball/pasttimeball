The Laredo Times of October 20, 1935 gave its readers fair warning: the Kansas City Monarchs "will make their first appearance in Nuevo Laredo Wednesday and Thursday of the following week, playing the La Junta ball club in two games." Both games at night, each "starting promptly at 8:30," by order of La Junta manager Erasmo Flores.

The paper wanted no illusions about what was coming. The Monarchs "compose a team which is by no means a pushover," having toured against the best clubs they could find and "always managing to come out ahead," so that "their name is synonymous with a winning ball club." And then the main event: "Their initial appearance here will feature a pitcher whose name is poison to opposing batters. He is Satchel Baige and will more than likely take La Junta for a ride if he pitches any of the games." The body of the story spells him Baige. The lineup at the bottom corrects the record with "the famous Satchel Paige, pitchers."

That lineup deserves reading in full: "Troupe and Young, catchers; Mayweather, first base; Allen, second base; Joseph third base; Bogen, left field; Dwight, center field; Milton right field; Brewer, Beverly, Cooper, Kransen Madison and the famous Satchel Paige, pitchers." Spellings are the compositor's own, but the names underneath are the real thing. Newt Allen at second and Newt Joseph at third anchored Monarchs infields for years, and Andy Cooper, one of the pitchers listed so casually there, has a plaque in Cooperstown now.

La Junta had a busy calendar that day. The same page has them [finishing a three-game showdown with Monterrey that very afternoon](/clippings/games-start-at-345-and-830/), and a few columns over, [a very large advertisement books their next opponents after that](/clippings/american-league-all-stars-vs-la-junta/). Count the days on a calendar and the Monarchs' "Wednesday and Thursday of the following week" lands on October 30 and 31, the exact two nights the ad promises Jimmy Foxx and the American League All-Stars at the same ball park. Somebody at the ball park had some sorting out to do.
