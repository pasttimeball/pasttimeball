Late in the summer of 1913 How Great Men Play the Game got to "Thomas Seaton, Star Pitcher, Philly Nationals," who was in the middle of the season of his life: 27 wins for the second-place Phillies, the most in the National League. Scar opens with the highest compliment the series had available. "Like Mathewson - Seaton has acquired a pitching poise which enables him to watch runners on first and third up to the moment the ball is put in play."

The mechanics note is brisk: "he pitches with a downward stroke of the arm and the ball leaves his hand with a sharp snappy wrist movement." Between the poise and the wrist, Scar draws him three times over, arms locked overhead, mid-delivery, then crouched and glaring in at whatever comes next.

The corner box describes the after-image better than a stat line could. "And did you ever notice? He pitches himself into an 'impenetrable net' or at least that's what it seems to the batter, trying to put a fast one through the pitchers box." A man so thoroughly finished with his follow-through that he has become a wall. Seaton jumped to the new Federal League that winter, so El Paso readers were looking at his best National League summer as it happened.
