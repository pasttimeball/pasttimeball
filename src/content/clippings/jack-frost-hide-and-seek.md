On April 29, 1918 the Omaha Daily Bee sent Frank Quigley out to cover a Sunday of amateur ball played in weather nobody ordered. "Evidently Jack Frost is playing hide and seek with the members of the Omaha Amateur Base Ball association, for it is a sure plunge he was in the neighborhood yesterday. Cool atmosphere conditions were responsible for the postponed games."

Some players stayed home, and Quigley had words for them: "Some of the cherry smashers are not in love with nursing a sore lunch hook and aching bones, consequently these sugar coated animals found a more appropriate place to spend their hours of leisure yesterday afternoon." But the balance were "hungry for base ball and Christmas weather does not toss them off the track," and the fans stood ready "to gobble up the brand of base ball dished up, even if it is necessary for them to jig around in their heavy togs in order to remain comfortable during the show."

The games that survived produced numbers worthy of the prose. The Sample Harts "danced over the pan with 26 runs, while the opposition gathered a quartet." A newcomer named McKeague, "a stranger in these woods, introduced himself by belting the pill to the remote corners of the lot." Pinault "twirled grand ball, keeping the slugging Trimbles low in the belt 'em column." And in the Intercity league the Daily News children rolled up 35 runs while the Graham Ice Creams hooked 4, which Quigley explained as only he could: "Well, ice cream doesn't travel at a fast clip in cool weather. Just wait until the torrid days roll around and the Graham Ice Creams will uncover the real article."
