The winter of 1929 belonged, briefly and loudly, to Arthur Shires, and Feg Murray's cartoon files the whole case on one panel: "Arthur (The Great) Shires, the 'Texas Bad Man,' who plays first base for the Chicago White Sox, and loves to fight." The Great was his own coinage, and he backed it in ways his employers did not always appreciate.

The main figure shows him doing his day job, folded over a grounder. The margins carry the rap sheet of assorted talents. Upper right, a punter in mid-follow-through: "He once kicked a 53-yard field goal in a high school game." Lower left, stripped to boxing trunks with his fists up: "He shoots a mean left hand!" A tiny cartoon citizen watches the shadowboxing and says the only thing there is to say: "My my my."

That mean left hand was current events. The column that ran beneath the cartoon catalogued a season of "numerous fights, two suspensions and a batting average of .312," including two rows with his own manager, Lena Blackburne. The best line in it concerns the brawl in which "Traveling Secretary Lou Barbour's thumb was badly bitten, but Shires explained that Blackburne did it by mistake, thinking it was his!" By December The Great had taken the obvious next step and turned professional in the ring, which is why a first baseman was getting the full-portrait treatment in the off season.

Murray drew for the Metropolitan Newspaper Service, so this portrait went wherever the syndicate's wires reached. The Evening Star ran it in Washington on December 12, 1929, on the same sports page where [an alderman was proposing to let school children into big league parks for a quarter](/clippings/big-leagues-may-admit-children-for-two-bits/). Baseball's winter news, then as now, ran the full range from the boardroom to the boxing ring.
