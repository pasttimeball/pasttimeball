The Laredo Times of October 20, 1935 gave over a third of a page to one word, BASEBALL, and the particulars underneath earned the type size. "Last Games of the Season. American League ALL-STARS vs. LA JUNTA. October 30 and 31, 8:30 P. M. NUEVO LAREDO BALL PARK." Then the sell: "See JIMMY FOXX, world's greatest home-run hitter, and GERALD WALKER, right fielder of the World Champion Detroit Tigers." The Tigers had finished off the Cubs in the World Series two weeks earlier, so that title was fresh from the factory.

The lineup card underneath is the kind you read twice. Rogers Hornsby at second base for the St. Louis Browns. Heinie Manush and Doc Cramer in the outfield. Pinky Higgins and Eric McNair on the left side of the infield, Ted Lyons, Earl Whitehill and Vernon Kennedy among the pitchers, and at the bottom, the finest job title ever set in agate: "Al Schacht, Coach comico: Boston Red Sox." Schacht was the Clown Prince of Baseball, and the ad translated him for the border trade.

One line in the fine print was ahead of the news. Foxx is listed "1B. Boston Red Sox," and in October 1935 that was not yet true. He was still Connie Mack's property, and the sale that sent him to Boston was not announced until December. Either the tour's promoter knew something, or the compositor made the deal six weeks early.

Admission ran "$3.00 Mex. Cy." for grandstand, $1.50 general, and "Ladies and Children HALF PRICE," which puts this ad in the honorable tradition of getting everybody to the park. La Junta earned the booking the hard way. That same morning the paper had them [deciding supremacy with Monterrey in a doubleheader](/clippings/games-start-at-345-and-830/), with [the Kansas City Monarchs and Satchel Paige announced for the same two nights as the All-Stars](/clippings/monarch-ball-club-plays-junta-soon/). Three good games, the bottom of the ad promises. Whoever showed up, it undersold.
