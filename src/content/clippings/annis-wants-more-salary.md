Spring 1909 brought the Daily Gate City this bulletin from the salary wars, headlined "Annis Wants More Salary." The sum in dispute is the best part: "William Annis, the shortstop of Wichita, for whom Anderson was traded, is holding out for more money. Thinking that a new place and a better one was in prospect, he wanted to boost his salary of last year by just exactly $25."

Twenty-five dollars. For the season. Management was unmoved: "President Higgins of the local club was not able to see his line of reasoning and immediately turned him down. Then Annis demanded his transportation, and this too was refused, so Annis is holding out," a holdout now conducted without train fare.

The paper saw no happy ending for the shortstop, noting the club's "great abundance of material" and that "the holdouts will not be treated with much mercy." Then it closed with geography: "Annis' contract will doubtless be coming to Des Moines within a day or two, and it is doubtful if he will walk the four hundred odd miles between here and Wichita." A hundred years of labor disputes since, and none has ever been summarized better than a man deciding whether $25 is worth a four hundred mile walk.
